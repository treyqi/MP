Chapter 12 Treatment Planning I: Isodose Distribution and Plan Evaluation

12.1 Penumbra

The dose distribution outside the field boundaries is significantly affected by geometric penumbra, depth, leakage radiation through collimator. The flattening filter mostly affect dose within the field boundary.

The disucssion of penumbra can be found in Section 5.3.

12.2 Wedges

  • Physical wedge
    • External physical wedge
    • Internal physical wedge (a.k.a motorized wedge, as in ElektaTM machines) typically consists of a single large wedge (e.g., 60 degrees) placed above the secondary collimating jaws. The wedge field is form by a combination of an open (o) field and a 60o wedge field:
\[\begin{equation} D_{\theta}=w_{O}D_{O} + w_{60^o}D_{60^o}, \tag{12.1} \end{equation}\]

\(\quad\quad\quad\quad\) where the weight \(w_{60^o}=\frac{tan\theta}{tan{60^o}}\).

  • Non-physical wedge
    • Virtual wedge (as in SiemensTM)
    • Enhanced dynamic wedge (EDW) in VarianTM, which is implemented by moving one of the collimating jaws from one end of the field to the other.

Wedge (isodose) angle is defined as the angle between wedged isodose curve (see figure wedge isodose) and the normal to the central axis at a specific depth (e.g., 10 cm). What we typically measure is wedge profile.

12.2.1 Wedge factors

According to Khan, the wedge (transmission) factor is defined as the ratios of dose measurement for a wedge field over that for an open field with the same field size and at the same depth along the central of the beam. It can be expressed as:

\[\begin{equation} \text{WF}=\frac{\text{D}_W(FS,d)}{\text{D}_O(FS,d)}. \end{equation}\]

The wedge factor required by the Pinnacle TPS is specifically defined as the ratio of the dose measurement for a wedge field and that for a open field (10 cm \(\times\) 10 cm) at the same depth of 10 cm (this is ICRU specified depth): \[\text{WF}_{Pinnacle} = \frac{\text{D}_W(FS, 10\ cm)}{\text{D}_O(FS_c, 10\ cm)},\] where the subscription c stands for calibration.10 The sample 6 MV WFpinnacle can be seen in Table 12.1 (form reference 3).

12.3.1 Integral dose

The integral dose is defined as the total energy imparted tot he patient and is given by the product of the mass of issue and the dos it receives. For photon external beam radiotherapy, the higher the energy, the lower the integral dose. For the same energy, different delivery techniques do not affect the integral dose much. If you want to spare certain organs, other organs will receive more doses as the integral dose is pretty much a constant. This is an important idea for you to remember when evaluating an IMRT/VMAT planif you wnat to spare nearby normally issues. The integral dose is an interesting concept but is has not been correlated to treatment outcome.

The cumulative DVH is a plot of the volume (y-axis) of a structure receiving a certain dose (x-axis) or higher. You need to find the differential (binned) DVH first, and then calculated cDVH. Shortcomings of DVHs

  1. does not provide spatial information of the dose in the targets or OARs
  2. accuracy is subject to the uncertainties in contouring, dose calculation, modeling.
  3. the dose to organ surface (e.g. bladder) is more important than to the volume.

Readers may find that the Point/Counterpoint article “Evaluation of treatment plans using target and normal tissue DVHs is no longer appropriate” and presentation “Treatment plan evaluation” are good read.
